## 1. Introduction

Urban sewer systems undertake the mission of residential and in-
dustrial wastewater and rainwater collection and transportation, and
they are an important part of urban infrastructure. In recent years,
sewer blocking, odor and corrosion problems have gradually aﬀected
the normal operation of urban sewers, and the physical and biological
reactions in sewer systems such as sediment accumulation (blocking)
and biological decomposition (odor and corrosion) have attracted more
attention by researchers [1–3]. Due to the diverse reactions that occur

Over the years, researchers have gradually realized that bioﬁlms
attached to sewer pipes have a non-negligible eﬀect on pollutant
transformation in sewage [7,8]. These studies showed that macro-
molecular organic matter in sewers could decompose into small mole-
cules by hydrolysis. Meanwhile, nitrogen and sulfur pollutants can be
degraded by diverse and functional microbial communities in the sewer
systems. In addition, there are also abundant microbial communities in
sediment, which can consume the substrates to generate methane and
hydrogen sulﬁde [2]. The metabolism of microbial communities will
induce the accumulation of degraded pollutants in sewage and sedi-
ment. Due to the porosity of the rough sediment surface, the pollutants
degraded by microbial communities in sewage and bioﬁlms can be
adsorbed by sediment [9], and this pollutant exchange pathway is re-
ferred to as biological transformation adsorption (BTA). Meanwhile, the
concentration of diverse degraded pollutants in sediment was much
higher than that in sewage [10]. Due to the apparent concentration
diﬀerence, the pollutant can also be released from sediment to sewage
[11], and this pollutant exchange pathway can be called biological
transformation release (BTR). Along with the pollutant deposition

## 2. Materials and methods

2.1. Operation of the pilot sewer system
In this study, a simulated 32-m long sewer system was constructed
as shown in the Supporting Fig. 1. The diameter of the sewer pipe was
200 mm. The sewage was collected from the urban sewer system in
Xi’an, China. It was raised to the top layer by a submersible pump, and
then ﬂowed to the bottom layer via gravity. The sewer pipes were in-
stalled with a slope of 5% to ensure a proper resistance coeﬃcient and
Reynolds number in the sewer system. The inner surface of the pipe was
polished to be rough. The total durations applied in this experiment
were 241 days. During this period, the formation process of sediment in
this pilot sewer system continued for ﬁrst 180 days. And then, the
characteristics of pollutant exchange between sewage and sediment
were analyzed after 180 days (for 61 days). In addition, to fully simu-
late the actual sewer networks in China, the experiment was conducted
at room temperature (25 ± 2 °C).
To evaluate the pollutant transformation pathways between sewage
and sediment, three experimental groups were conducted under dif-
ferent conditions (as shown in the Supporting Fig. 2). Diﬀerent ex-
perimental materials and control methods were utilized in the three
experiments. Real sewage and real sediment were used in the ﬁrst ex-
periment without any control methods to simulate the actual sewer
systems. In the second experiment, the biological metabolism in sewage
was prohibited by placing ﬁve ultraviolet lamps in the storage tank of
the sewage to inactivate the microbial communities. The sterilized
sewage was well stirred before raising it to the top layer of the sewer
system. In addition, the bioﬁlms attached to the inner surface of the
sewer pipes were removed. Thus, the BTA pathway could be cut oﬀ.
And then, real sewage and kaolin-quartz sand were used in the third
experiment. The ingredients of kaolin-quartz sediment are shown in
Table 1. The prepared kaolin-quartz sediment could highly simulate the
real sediment and the BTR pathway could be cut oﬀin this group of
experiment. Furthermore, the methane and sulfate concentrations were
detected in the pilot system with no sediment to evaluate the generation
ability of bioﬁlm.

## 4. Conclusions

In this study, the pollutant exchange pathways between urban
sewage and sediment were studied in a pilot sewer system. During the
long-term monitoring, the pollutant concentration decreased in urban
sewage and became enriched in sediment, while degraded matters
(VFA) was increased by 20.3 mg-COD/L in sewage. In addition, the
relative abundance of the hydrolytic microbial community increased
from 0.76% to 2.89% from day 30 to day 180, and it could also provide
a suitable environment for the release of pollutant from sediment.
Based on the characteristics of physical and biological reactions in
sewer, the pathways between sewage and sediment, namely, PPD, BTA
and BTR were proposed for the ﬁrst time. Among the three pathways,
the physical deposition values of organic, nitrogen and phosphorus
pollutants were the highest which transferred 144.0 mg/L, 5.15 mg/L
and 3.79 mg/L between sewage and sediment, respectively. The result
showed that the PPD was the dominant process of pollutants transfor-
mation in urban sewer systems. Meanwhile, the values in BTR were
39.2 mg/L for organic matters, 1.79 mg/L for nitrogen pollutants and
2.12 mg/L for phosphorus pollutants which much higher than those in
BTA (14.7 mg/L; 1.79 mg/L and 2.12 mg/L, respectively). This char-
acteristics can signiﬁcantly induce a signiﬁcant change of biodegrad-
ability in sewage. Based on those results, an in-depth understanding of
pollutant exchange characteristics between sewage and sediment can
provide a reliable guideline for future development of urban sewer
systems.
